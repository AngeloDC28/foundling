I. Curatorial Statement and Aims

This immersive installation aims to unfold as a journey rather than a fixed narrative. Moving through the space, audiences encounter fragments of lives at the Foundling Hospital drawn from both historical archives and contemporary care leavers’ experiences. It reflects on the archive not as a stable record, acknowledging that telling the past involves choices: what is preserved, what is omitted, and how stories are framed.

Through sound, spatial design, and interaction, the piece proposes a way of engaging with these histories in a responsible, creative, critical manner: the testimonies (voices, archival documents, objects and belongings) grouped and displaced across the room do not unfold linearly. Walking around, between and through the different extracts/stories, pressing on sampler pads to literally activate fragments of voices (almost as an experimental chorus) allow the audience itself to interpret them, and then bring them into relation with one another.

This event invites visitors to move through the room with agency, in order to individually and collectively create a site of thinking around systems and institutions of care as the audience acts as an active participant in assembling meaning. Each voice, once triggered, enters into a shifting dialogue with others, creating a layered and evolving sound environment. Past and present are no longer experienced as separate temporalities, but as coexisting presences and absences that inform and challenge one another through space and sound. Rather than reconstructing a complete story, the installation foregrounds fragmentation, absence, and multiplicity.

II. Strategic Concept and Core Questions

We define a "foundling" as an infant abandoned by its parents and discovered and cared for by others; yet we question the quality and depth of that "care" across centuries. The strategic goal is to examine how systems of care have historically prioritised the production of functional, "useful citizens" over the emotional recognition of the individual.

This project treats every indexed runway and archival document as a cultural artifact; isolating the moments where institutional discipline subverts bodily autonomy. We are not merely looking at the past; we are activating a "chorus across time" where the audience functions as the mediator, bringing archival absences into the acoustic present.

Core Inquiries:

  • The Paradox of "Looking After": What does it truly mean to be "looked after" within an institutional framework? We examine the tension between the Foundling Hospital’s aim to rescue "cruelly wasted lives" and the actualised reality of rescue via discipline and usefulness.
  • The Architecture of Preparation: What specific, relational forms of care are required to prepare a subject for the world? We audit the transition from the regulated life of a "total institution" to the "unstructured autonomy" of adulthood; identifying where relational maps are missing.
  • Survival vs. Recognition: What is the psychological cost when a system provides physical structure and material survival, such as food and uniforms, but negates emotional development and individual interiority? This inquiry explores the formation of the "false self"—a competent shell that performs stability while lacking internal cohesion.
  • The Reparative Archive: How can the archive be activated as a site of repair for those it historically silenced? By placing administrative logs and apprenticeship records in direct dialogue with contemporary vocal testimonies.
  • Contemporary Echoes: To what extent do historical logics of functional care persist in modern systems? We compare the 18th-century removal of birth names to the modern indignity of care leavers being given black bin bags for their belongings; questioning if the structural absence of dignity is a fundamental institutional trait.

III. The Bureaucratic Trajectory: 1739 to Present

Historical care at the Foundling Hospital operated as a rigorous exercise in social engineering. The institutional mission was the radical transformation of society's "unwanted" babies into "worthy" or "useful citizens". This required the systematic erasure of original identity; birth names were deleted and replaced with administrative labels to safeguard maternal anonymity and break biological ties.

1739: The Royal Charter and Forensic Marking

Thomas Coram secured a Royal Charter in 1739. This charter legally enshrined the practice of renaming every child upon admission, a protocol designed to protect the "character" of the mothers who relinquished them.

  • The Lottery System: Due to limited capacity, admission was initially decided by a lottery where mothers drew colored balls from a bag; a white ball meant admission, a red ball indicated a place on the waiting list, and a black ball meant immediate rejection.
  • The Admission Billet: Upon entry, a forensic "billet" was generated to record the child's sex, estimated age, and a detailed inventory of their clothing.
  • Unique Numbering: Every baby was assigned a unique admittance number, which was stamped onto a lead tag and hung around the child's neck as a permanent administrative marker.
  • Maternal Tokens: Mothers often left small physical objects or swatches of fabric as identifying "tokens". These were pinned to the admission billet and sealed, remaining unopened unless the parent returned to prove kinship through a physical match.

1800s: The Apprenticeship and Gendered Utility

By the 19th century, the curriculum was specifically designed to prepare foundlings for a "servile and laborious" station in life. Discipline and routine were the primary tools used to instill the work ethic required for their designated social roles.

  • Boys’ Apprenticeships: Boys were trained in manual trades such as husbandry, clockmaking, or blacksmithing. A significant number were funneled into the "sea service" or the British Army.
  • The Military Band: In 1847, the introduction of a military style boys' band provided a new professional pathway, training foundlings as musicians for the armed forces.
  • Girls’ Apprenticeships: Opportunities for girls were strictly limited to domestic service or "household business". They were trained in tambour embroidery, weaving, and dressmaking to prepare them for roles as servants in households across the country.
  • Supervision until 21: The Hospital maintained legal authority over these young adults until the age of 21 for girls and up to 24 for boys, conducting regular inspections of their workplaces to ensure "good character" and sobriety.

1948: The Shift to Family Care

Post-war changes in child psychology highlighted the critical importance of emotional attachment over rigid institutional stability. The 1948 Children Act revolutionized public provision by placing family-based care at the center of the system.

  • School Closure: In light of this legislative shift, the Foundling Hospital closed its residential school in 1954.
  • Internal Restructuring: A new Children's Department was established, staffed by qualified child care specialists and social workers tasked with finding foster families rather than managing a dormitory system.

1989 and 2004: Best Interests and Safeguarding

The Children Acts of 1989 and 2004 fundamentally transformed child welfare by prioritizing the safety and welfare of the child above all other considerations. The law introduced the concept of "Parental Responsibility" and the 2004 Act mandated inter-agency cooperation.

2012: Concurrent Planning and Attachment Security

In 2012, Coram pioneered "Concurrent Planning" to address the inherent instability found in traditional foster-care proceedings, working toward Plan A (reunification) and Plan B (adoption) simultaneously.

IV. Methodological Framework: Theoretical Pillars

Our intervention is built upon a rigorous academic foundation that treats the archive as a site of both absence and potential repair. We move beyond passive observation to perform a forensic audit of institutional power and its impact on the individual subject.

Voices, Rights, and Future Page 7

The "False Self" and the Total Institution

  • Donald Winnicott (1960) and the Competent Shell: We utilise the concept of the "false self" to analyse how children in the system developed personality structures aimed at adaptation rather than authentic expression. In environments where adaptation replaces authenticity, individuals become "competent shells"; adults who perform stability and functionality while lacking internal cohesion. As Alice Heskett noted, one could be "looked after wonderful" in a material sense while remaining entirely "frightened" and "not knowing a thing" about the world outside.
  • Erving Goffman (1961) and Structural Dislocation: The Foundling Hospital operated as a "total institution" where identity was reshaped through structured environments that regulated every aspect of autonomy. The routines of the institution—including identical uniforms and strict timetables—regulated time and space to the point of erasing the individual. The shock of "institutional exit" often left individuals without internalized maps for navigating choice; the resulting unsettlement is not theatrical confusion but a profound structural dislocation.

Archive and Power: Reanimation Strategies

  • Stuart Hall (2001) and Constructed Narratives: We view the archive as a constructed narrative produced by those in power rather than a neutral historical record. Our work seeks to "constitute" the archive anew by bringing overlooked voices into the present acoustic space.
  • Saidiya Hartman (2008) and the Administrative Trace: We recognize the "violence of the archive" in its reduction of lived-experience to partial, administrative traces like billets and numbers. Through "reanimation strategies," we address these gaps to reattribute emotional lives to those once rendered invisible by the institution's desire for "useful citizens".
  • Michelle Caswell (2021) and the Ethics of Care: We mobilise the archive as a site of care and repair; actively working to restore visibility and recognition to lives that were historically limited by institutional frameworks.

V. Materiality and Absence: Artifact Analysis

The installation utilises specific objects to bridge the centuries between the foundling experience and the modern care leaver; isolating the garments and items that symbolise societal codes of conformity and neglect.

The Military Vest vs. The Coat Hanger

Institutional Ownership: Foundling boys often transitioned directly from school uniforms into Army uniforms. John Darrell’s testimony reveals that they possessed no civilian clothes; they were moved from one state-owned uniform to another without choice.

The Production of Uniformity: This lack of "civilian clothing" symbolized a life perpetually owned by the state where the body was treated as a functional tool for the military or domestic service. In contrast, our display features modern jackets on deco hangers alongside contemporary testimonies. This reflects the modern care leaver’s relationship to dress as a tool for forming identity after exiting a system where choices were not their own.

The Empty Basket vs. The Black Bin Bag

The Hatch of Abandonment: Historically, the "hatch" or basket was the physical site of abandonment at the Foundling Hospital; it was the moment a child was reduced to an administrative billet.

Disposable Autonomy: Today, testimonies from care leavers highlight a recurring modern indignity; being given black bin bags to pack their entire lives when transitioning to "unstructured autonomy". One interviewee recounted being given two bin bags at age 17, taken to a youth hostel, and never seeing their social worker again.

VI. Testimony Analysis: The Paradox of Provision

The interactive sampler pads function as forensic triggers; they allow the audience to pull individual interiority out of a generic institutional record that traditionally prioritised silence. These specific fragments were selected to highlight the persistent tension between "functional care" (material survival) and "emotional neglect" (relational abandonment).

"I used to stay in my bedroom and cry my eyes out. I was too frightened to go out, 'cause not knowing anybody and I didn't know about the world."
— Alice Heskett

Her testimony isolates the "institutional exit shock"; it proves that while the system "clothed and fed" the children, it sent them into the "big wide world" without a relational map or a sense of private self.

"The practice was that... bandmasters... would visit... You had no choice. You didn't speak to the bandmaster visiting."
— Sydney Ansell

This fragment decodes the mechanics of the "Total Institution" where the child is treated as a moldable asset for military utility rather than a person with agency.

"We went from school uniform straight into Army uniform."
— John Darrell

This literal transition of clothing symbolizes a life perpetually owned by state structures; it suggests that for the foundling, "civilian life" was a state they were never permitted to fully inhabit.

VII. Technical and Sonic Setup: The Architecture of Listening

The installation utilises an integrated digital and physical architecture to facilitate a collective act of listening; one that treats sound as a reparative tool rather than mere background ambiance.

  • Hardware Interface: The audience interacts with two Akai MIDI pad controllers connected to a central laptop via a multi-output audio interface. This tactile setup encourages the physical "activation" of the archive by the visitor.
  • Spatial Configuration: The space uses a triangular layout with speakers positioned far apart to create a mild sense of spatial disorientation. This mimics the anxiety of "unstructured autonomy" described by foundlings upon leaving the hospital gates.
  • Software and Sidechain Logic: Ableton Live manages a continuous ambient loop of mechanical institutional sounds; such as cutlery on plates and distant marching rhythms. Whenever a testimony pad is triggered, this loop "ducks" in volume via sidechain compression.
  • Metaphor of the "Dip": This sonic "dip" is a deliberate curatorial strategy; it forces the institutional noise to recede so the individual voice can briefly break through. The audience does not simply listen; they participate in bringing the archive to life by manually surfacing the hidden interiority of the subjects.
  • The Evolving Chorus: As multiple pads are pressed, a layered composition forms a "dispersed and evolving chorus". This Acoustic Territory allows voices separated by centuries to share the same acoustic space; forming a temporary community of recognition that breaks down the barriers of the traditional archive.

IX. Structural Limitations and Risks

  • The 10% Hard Wall: Allocating £100 to contingency (10% of the total budget) is a non-negotiable risk management strategy. This fund specifically protects the student group against the full replacement cost of CSM loan store items in the event of major damage.
  • Production Constraints: Reducing the "Printing and Production" and "Research" categories requires the group to rely more heavily on digital-first strategies and hand-made archival recreations.
  • Labor Pressure: The reduction in the "Staffing" budget to £120 increases the manual labor requirements for the core student team, who do not receive wages from this fund.
  • Catering Bottleneck: The £130 hospitality fund remains a contributor-only resource and cannot support a public reception.
  • Institutional Dependency: The project’s feasibility relies entirely on the Foundling Museum’s infrastructure; any withdrawal of venue support would render the project unviable.

X. Annotated Bibliography

On ARCHIVE

  • Stuart Hall — Constituting an Archive (2001)
    As they aren’t neutral collections of the past, archives are shaped by decisions regarding what is kept, what is excluded, how things are organised. In this matter, they don’t just store history, they actively construct how we look at it and interpret it.
  • Saidiya Hartman — Venus in Two Acts (2008)
    Archives never fully tell the lives they record as they reduce people to fragments, often administrative traces, and leave a lot unsaid. Hartman insists on working with these gaps, notably by acknowledging absence instead of trying to fill it completely.
  • Michelle Caswell — Urgent Archives (2021)
    Caswell argues that archives can move beyond preservation to become tools for care, recognition, and social justice. She focuses on how communities can reclaim and reshape archives to repair histories that were neglected or misrepresented. Archives can be understood as active spaces of responsibility, not just storage.
  • Okwui Enwezor — Archive Fever (2008)
    Contemporary artists treat archives as material to be reworked rather than preserved as stable truth. Through rearrangement, fragmentation, and juxtaposition, archives become unstable and open to new meanings. The focus shifts from what the archive contains to how it is used and activated.

On SOUND

  • Salomé Voegelin — Listening to Noise and Silence (2010)
    Listening isn’t passive. We don’t just receive sound: we produce meaning through it. As sound doesn’t carry a fixed message, it shifts depending on context, attention, and experience. This signifies that every listening situation is unstable, open, and slightly different for each person.
  • Brandon LaBelle — Acoustic Territories (2010)
    Sound shapes how we experience space and how we relate to others within it. You don’t necessarily need direct interaction, listening in the same environment already creates a shared condition. It produces a kind of temporary, subtle and intangible community.
  • Nina Sun Eidsheim — The Race of Sound (2019)
    A voice is never only a sound or words: it is embedded in identity, history, and social meaning. Moreover, what we hear is shaped by perception, expectation, and context. Listening can therefore never be seen as neutral, it is always an interpretative and subjective act.
  • Susan Philipsz — Lowlands (2010)
    Her work uses recorded voice without a visible body, which creates a strong sense of absence. This would have had the effect of amplifying the sense of disembodiment of the voice(s) which feels present, but also incomplete: like a trace of someone no longer there. This produces a quiet, haunting relationship to space and memory.
  • Janet Cardiff & George Bures Miller — The Forty-Part Motet (2001)
    They separate voices in space so you can walk between them. In their words, this enables the audience to “move throughout the space, allowing them to be intimately connected with the voices. It also reveals the piece as a changing construct”. It also reflects on “how sound may physically construct a space in a sculptural way and how a viewer may choose a path through this physical yet virtual space”.

The event is constructed around bringing these ideas together. The archive is structured/structural and incomplete, while sound is experiential and relational. Thus, the installation becomes a space where listening acts as a counter/ reparative strategies of reanimation to recompose and create a lived archive, in a participatory and attentive reconstruction of memory.
