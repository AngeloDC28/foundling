I. Curatorial Statement and Aims

This immersive installation functions as a non-linear trajectory through the administrative silences of the Foundling Hospital. Moving through the site, audiences encounter fragments of interiority—drawn from both historical records and contemporary testimonies—reanimated within a space that rejects stable narrative resolution. We treat the archive not as a passive repository, but as a site of active construction where telling the past necessitates an audit of preservation, omission, and framing.

Our audience strategy intentionally disrupts passive consumption. The testimonies (voices, archival documents, objects, and belongings) distributed across the room do not unfold linearly. The audience is positioned as an active mediator. By navigating the space and physically pressing the MIDI sampler pads, the audience literally activates fragments of voices, forming an experimental acoustic chorus.

This event invites visitors to move through the room with agency, individually and collectively creating a site of critical reflection. The installation relies on the audience to assemble meaning; without their physical interaction, the archive remains silent.

II. Strategic Concept and Core Questions

We define a "foundling" as an infant abandoned by its parents and discovered and cared for by others; yet we audit the quality of that "care" across centuries. Our strategic objective is to examine the paradox of the "total institution": a system that prioritises the production of functional, "useful citizens" whilst simultaneously negating the emotional interiority of the individual.

This project treats every indexed record and archival document as a cultural artefact; isolating the moments where institutional discipline subverts bodily autonomy. We are not merely displaying the past; we are reanimating a "chorus across time" where the audience functions as the mediator, bringing archival absences into the acoustic present through physical triggers.

Core Inquiries:

  • The Paradox of "Functional Care": What does it truly mean to be "looked after" within an institutional framework? We examine the tension between the Foundling Hospital’s mission to rescue "cruelly wasted lives" and the actualised reality of material survival contingent upon emotional neglect.
  • The Architecture of Preparation: What specific, relational forms of care are required to prepare a subject for the world? We audit the transition from the regulated life of a "total institution" to the "unstructured autonomy" of adulthood; identifying where relational maps were systematically withheld.
  • Survival vs. Recognition: We analyse the psychological cost when a system provides material structure—food, shelter, and uniforms—whilst negating emotional development. This inquiry utilises Winnicott's "false self" to explore the formation of a competent shell that performs stability whilst lacking internal cohesion.
  • The Reparative Archive: How can the archive be reanimated as a site of repair for those it historically silenced? By placing administrative logs in direct sidechained dialogue with contemporary vocal testimonies, we surface the hidden agency of the subject.
  • Contemporary Echoes: To what extent do historical logics of functional care persist? We compare the 18th-century forensic "billet" to the modern indignity of care leavers utilising black bin bags for their belongings; questioning if the structural absence of dignity is a fundamental institutional trait.

III. The Bureaucratic Trajectory: 1739 to Present

Historical care at the Foundling Hospital operated as a rigorous exercise in social engineering. The institutional mission was the radical transformation of society's "unwanted" babies into "worthy" or "useful citizens". This required the systematic erasure of original identity; birth names were deleted and replaced with administrative labels to safeguard maternal anonymity and break biological ties.

1739: The Royal Charter and Forensic Marking

Thomas Coram secured a Royal Charter in 1739. This charter legally enshrined the practice of renaming every child upon admission, a protocol designed to protect the "character" of the mothers who relinquished them.

  • The Lottery System: Due to limited capacity, admission was initially decided by a lottery where mothers drew coloured balls from a bag; a white ball meant admission, a red ball indicated a place on the waiting list, and a black ball meant immediate rejection.
  • The Admission Billet: Upon entry, a forensic "billet" was generated to record the child's sex, estimated age, and a detailed inventory of their clothing—the first administrative step in identity erasure.
  • Unique Numbering: Every baby was assigned a unique admittance number, stamped onto a lead tag and hung around the child's neck as a permanent administrative marker.
  • Maternal Tokens: Mothers often left small physical objects or swatches of fabric as identifying "tokens". These were pinned to the admission billet and sealed, remaining unopened unless the parent returned to prove kinship through a physical match—a rare moment of archival reanimation.

1800s: The Apprenticeship and Gendered Utility

By the 19th century, the curriculum was specifically designed to prepare foundlings for a "servile and laborious" station in life. Discipline and routine were the primary tools used to instill the work ethic required for their designated social roles.

  • Boys’ Apprenticeships: Boys were trained in manual trades such as husbandry, clockmaking, or blacksmithing. A significant number were funneled into the "sea service" or the British Army.
  • The Military Band: In 1847, the introduction of a military style boys' band provided a new professional pathway, training foundlings as musicians for the armed forces.
  • Girls’ Apprenticeships: Opportunities for girls were strictly limited to domestic service or "household business". They were trained in tambour embroidery, weaving, and dressmaking to prepare them for roles as servants in households across the country.
  • Supervision until 21: The Hospital maintained legal authority over these young adults until the age of 21 for girls and up to 24 for boys, conducting regular inspections of their workplaces to ensure "good character" and sobriety.

1948: The Shift to Family Care

Post-war changes in child psychology highlighted the critical importance of emotional attachment over rigid institutional stability. The 1948 Children Act revolutionized public provision by placing family-based care at the center of the system.

  • School Closure: In light of this legislative shift, the Foundling Hospital closed its residential school in 1954, moving away from the dormitory-based "total institution" model.
  • Internal Restructuring: A new Children's Department was established, staffed by qualified child care specialists tasked with prioritising foster families over rigid administrative management.

1989 and 2004: Best Interests and Safeguarding

The Children Acts of 1989 and 2004 fundamentally transformed child welfare by prioritizing the safety and welfare of the child above all other considerations. The law introduced the concept of "Parental Responsibility" and the 2004 Act mandated inter-agency cooperation.

2012: Concurrent Planning and Attachment Security

In 2012, Coram pioneered "Concurrent Planning" to address the inherent instability found in traditional foster-care proceedings, working toward Plan A (reunification) and Plan B (adoption) simultaneously.

IV. Methodological Framework: Theoretical Pillars

Our intervention is built upon a rigorous academic foundation that treats the archive as a site of both absence and potential repair. We move beyond passive observation to perform a forensic audit of institutional power and its impact on the individual subject.

Voices, Rights, and Future Page 7

The "False Self" and the Total Institution

  • Donald Winnicott (1960) and the Competent Shell: We utilise the concept of the "false self" to analyse how children in the system developed personality structures aimed at adaptation rather than authentic expression. In environments where functional care replaces emotional recognition, individuals become "competent shells"; adults who perform stability while lacking internal cohesion. As former foundling Alice Heskett noted, one could be "looked after wonderful" in a material sense (food, shelter) while remaining entirely "frightened" and "not knowing a thing" about navigating the outside world.
  • Erving Goffman (1961) and the "Total Institution": The Foundling Hospital functioned as a "total institution" where identity was systematically reshaped through regulated environments. The routines—identical uniforms, lead markers, and strict timetables—erased the individual in favour of the administrative subject. The shock of "institutional exit" often left individuals without an internalised compass for navigating unstructured autonomy; a dislocation we sonically mimic through spatial disorientation.

Archive and Power: Reanimation Strategies

  • Stuart Hall (2001) and Constructed Narratives: We view the archive as a constructed narrative produced by those in power rather than a neutral historical record. Our work seeks to "constitute" the archive anew by bringing overlooked voices into the present acoustic space.
  • Saidiya Hartman (2008) and the Administrative Trace: We recognize the "violence of the archive" in its reduction of lived-experience to partial, administrative traces like billets and numbers. Through "reanimation strategies," we address these gaps to reattribute emotional lives to those once rendered invisible by the institution's desire for "useful citizens".
  • Michelle Caswell (2021) and the Ethics of Care: We mobilise the archive as a site of care and repair; actively working to restore visibility and recognition to lives that were historically limited by institutional frameworks.

V. Materiality and Absence: Archival Reanimation

The installation utilises specific objects to bridge the centuries between the foundling experience and the modern care leaver. By engaging directly with the London Archives, we isolate the material artefacts that symbolise societal codes of institutional conformity.

  • The Military Vest vs. The Coat Hanger: Foundling boys often transitioned directly from school uniforms into Army uniforms (as evidenced by Apprenticeship Registers, John Darrell’s testimony reveals that they possessed no civilian clothes. This lack of "civilian clothing" symbolised a life perpetually owned by the state. In contrast, our display features modern jackets on deco hangers, reflecting the contemporary care leaver’s effort to form an autonomous identity after exiting a system where aesthetic choices were heavily regulated.
  • The Empty Basket vs. The Black Bin Bag: Historically, the "hatch" or basket was the physical site of abandonment; the moment a child was reduced to an administrative billet (Ref: Billet 2,767, 1756). Today, testimonies highlight a recurring modern indignity: being given black bin bags to pack their entire lives when transitioning to hostels at age 17. This material consistency across centuries highlights a structural failure to provide dignity. The bin bag serves as a symbol of "disposable autonomy."

VI. Testimony Analysis: The Paradox of Provision

The interactive MIDI pads function as forensic triggers; they allow the audience to pull individual interiority out of an administrative record that historically prioritised silence. These fragments isolate the tension between "functional care" (material survival) and "emotional neglect" (relational abandonment), reanimating the subject as a co-creator with agency.

"I used to stay in my bedroom and cry my eyes out. I was too frightened to go out, 'cause not knowing anybody and I didn't know about the world."
— Alice Heskett

Her testimony isolates the "institutional exit shock"; it proves that while the system "clothed and fed" the children, it sent them into the "big wide world" without a relational map or a sense of private self.

"The practice was that... bandmasters... would visit... You had no choice. You didn't speak to the bandmaster visiting."
— Sydney Ansell

This fragment decodes the mechanics of the "Total Institution" where the child is treated as a moldable asset for military utility rather than a person with agency.

"We went from school uniform straight into Army uniform."
— John Darrell

This literal transition of clothing symbolizes a life perpetually owned by state structures; it suggests that for the foundling, "civilian life" was a state they were never permitted to fully inhabit.

VII. Technical and Sonic Setup: The Architecture of Listening

The installation utilises an integrated digital and physical architecture to facilitate a collective act of listening; one that treats sound as a reparative tool rather than mere background ambiance.

  • Hardware Interface: Audiences function as "mediators" via two Akai MIDI controllers. This tactile setup necessitates the physical "activation" of the archive, moving beyond passive observation to a state of active reanimation.
  • Spatial Configuration: The site uses a triangular layout with speakers positioned far apart to create a mild spatial disorientation, mimicking the "exit shock" experienced when moving from a total institution to unstructured autonomy.
  • Sidechain Logic: Ableton Live manages a continuous ambient loop of mechanical institutional noise (metronomic rhythms and administrative sounds). When a testimony pad is triggered, this loop is sidechained—dynamically "ducking" in volume to allow the individual voice to surface.
  • Metaphor of the "Dip": This sonic "dip" is a deliberate curatorial strategy; it forces institutional power to recede so the individual voice can briefly break through. The audience does not simply listen; they participate in manually surfacing the hidden interiority of the co-creators.
  • The Evolving Chorus: As multiple pads are triggered, a layered composition forms an "Acoustic Territory." This allows voices separated by centuries to share the same acoustic space, constituting a temporary community of recognition that breaks the silence of the archive.

Ethics of Care and Safeguarding

This intervention operates under a rigorous ethical framework designed to protect vulnerable participants and prevent the proliferation of trauma narratives. We actively reject the framing of care leavers as victims; instead, they are engaged as co-creators with agency over their representation.

  • Curatorial Safeguarding: We have made a strict curatorial decision to engage exclusively with adult care leavers (ages 18 to 30). Because our inquiry focuses on the psychological shock of entering unstructured autonomy, engaging directly with minors introduces complex safeguarding risks that fall outside the parameters of this adult-centric activation.
  • Anonymity and Consent: All contemporary testimonies are sourced through direct dialogue with advocacy networks like the Care Leavers' Association. Contributors retain the right to remain anonymous to prevent online identification.
  • Ethical Compensation: We recognise that lived experience is a valuable form of expertise. Despite the limitations of a student budget, the provision of honorariums for our speakers is a non-negotiable ethical baseline, ensuring our collaborative practice remains extractive-free.

IX. Ethics of Care: Safeguarding and Agency

  • Safeguarding Parameters: We have restricted our contemporary engagement to adults aged 18-30 who have already exited the care system. This decision ensures that participants have the necessary unstructured autonomy to reflect on their experiences without compromising their ongoing relationship with active social work teams.
  • Co-creation vs. Representation: We reject the framing of care leavers as "victims" or passive subjects. Instead, they are compensated through honorariums as expert co-creators. Their voices are not "displayed"; they are reanimated by the audience as mediators in a shared acoustic space.
  • The 10% Risk Wall: Allocating £100 to contingency is a non-negotiable strategy to protect the student team against the full replacement cost of CSM loan store items, ensuring the feasibility of our high-value institutional currency model.

X. Annotated Bibliography

On ARCHIVE

  • Stuart Hall — Constituting an Archive (2001)
    Hall argues that archives aren’t neutral collections of the past; they are constructed through decisions of exclusion and organisation. In this matter, they don’t just store history; they actively prioritise certain narratives whilst silencing others.
  • Saidiya Hartman — Venus in Two Acts (2008)
    Archives never fully tell the lives they record as they reduce people to fragments, often administrative traces, and leave a lot unsaid. Hartman insists on working with these gaps, notably by acknowledging absence instead of trying to fill it completely.
  • Michelle Caswell — Urgent Archives (2021)
    Caswell argues that archives can move beyond preservation to become tools for care, recognition, and social justice. She focuses on how communities can reclaim and reshape archives to repair histories that were neglected or misrepresented. Archives can be understood as active spaces of responsibility, not just storage.
  • Okwui Enwezor — Archive Fever (2008)
    Contemporary artists treat archives as material to be reworked rather than preserved as stable truth. Through rearrangement, fragmentation, and juxtaposition, archives become unstable and open to new meanings. The focus shifts from what the archive contains to how it is used and activated.

On SOUND

  • Salomé Voegelin — Listening to Noise and Silence (2010)
    Listening isn’t passive. We don’t just receive sound: we produce meaning through it. As sound doesn’t carry a fixed message, it shifts depending on context, attention, and experience. This signifies that every listening situation is unstable, open, and slightly different for each person.
  • Brandon LaBelle — Acoustic Territories (2010)
    Sound shapes how we experience space and how we relate to others within it. You don’t necessarily need direct interaction, listening in the same environment already creates a shared condition. It produces a kind of temporary, subtle and intangible community.
  • Nina Sun Eidsheim — The Race of Sound (2019)
    A voice is never only a sound or words: it is embedded in identity, history, and social meaning. Moreover, what we hear is shaped by perception, expectation, and context. Listening can therefore never be seen as neutral, it is always an interpretative and subjective act.
  • Susan Philipsz — Lowlands (2010)
    Her work uses recorded voice without a visible body, which creates a strong sense of absence. This would have had the effect of amplifying the sense of disembodiment of the voice(s) which feels present, but also incomplete: like a trace of someone no longer there. This produces a quiet, haunting relationship to space and memory.
  • Janet Cardiff & George Bures Miller — The Forty-Part Motet (2001)
    They separate voices in space so you can walk between them. In their words, this enables the audience to “move throughout the space, allowing them to be intimately connected with the voices. It also reveals the piece as a changing construct”. It also reflects on “how sound may physically construct a space in a sculptural way and how a viewer may choose a path through this physical yet virtual space”.

This curatorial intervention integrates the structural density of the archive with the relational potential of sound. By acknowledging the archive as incomplete, the installation creates a space where listening acts as a counter-reparative strategy of reanimation. We recompose a lived archive through a participatory reconstruction of memory, transforming the audience into mediators of a shared, forensic truth.
